Identity, Values and Strengths Exploration

This topic was an interesting one for me. As someone who doesn't say much, I haven't spent much time thinking about what my values are. There were plenty of light bulb moments during the exploration stage and I'm happy to share what I found.

2 March 2025

VIA Survey of Character Strengths

  1. Humour and playfulness
  2. Fairness, equity, justice
  3. Gratitude
  4. Curiosity and interest in the world
  5. Honest, authenticity and genuineness

From the list above, I see myself very clearly. This strengths test was the first thing I did when starting exploring this topic. It opened my eyes and gave me a better prespective on what I value in this life.

Gallup Strengths Finder

  1. Adaptability
  2. Empathy
  3. Posivitity
  4. Developer

What I found really interesting about the strengths finder test is, I've done this test 3 times in the past 10 years and my top five have always remained the same, except for one. Developer is a new strength in my list. In my most recent test, Developer has replaced Responsibility.

Rebecca is an adventurous, funny, creative, delightful woman. Rebecca is determined to achieve what ever she puts her mind to. When she is working on problems in her job she endevours to find solutions rather than leaving work for collegues.
Rebecca prefers working in an environment that fosters positive relationships, this is reflected in her values of authenticity and humour. Rebecca finds humour a vehicle to relax herself and others in social situations, the humour is situational and self depreciating.
The courage Rebecca shows is evident in being willing to try new opportunities and to set goals for herself. In situations that are new to her she sets to challenge herself. This is evident in the course that she is now enrolled in and entering middle distance running events..
Rebecca is creative and will find artistic ways to express her creativity with resources she has available.
Rebecca thinks deeply with empathy when thinking about what is happening around her and in the world. This sometimes leads to her not being able to share her opinion in case she offends people, and to keep the peace. - a testimony from my mother, Kathryn.

I've known Rebecca for 8 years (as a my best friend and now wife) and I can honestly say she’s one of the most hardworking and dedicated people that I know. No matter what she’s working on, she gives it her true effort. Her strong work ethic and punctuality makes her very reliable and you can always count on her to show up and get things done.
She’s smart (and street smart), creative, and a team player, always bringing fresh ideas to the table.
Even though she can be a little shy at times, she has a lighthearted side that makes her so fun to be around. She loves to joke around and keep things positive.
On top of all that, Rebecca is incredibly kind, caring, and accommodating- always going out of her way to help others and make sure everyone feels included. Her loyalty and support mean the world to those lucky enough to know them.
Rebecca responds well to positive feedback and support. There are moments when she may doubt her judgement, but I know she will benefit deeply from exposing herself to new experiences, make more mistakes and be fearless. - a testimony from my husband, Ninad.

Explain a situation where you have made an ethical decision. Discuss how you weighed up the values involved in that decision, the decision you made, and how you reflect on the decision now.

A group of friends and I went to Pak'n Save to pick up some ingredients for a youth group bake-off. We went through the self-checkout (this was back in the early 2010's, when self-checkout just became popular in Palmerston North). In our trolley we had the basic cake ingredients; flour, cocoa, icing sugar and a few other out of the box ingredients such as lemons. We checked out our items and were about to leave the store and I looked down into our trolley and saw a lemon that had not been put in our shopping bags. We checked the receipt and realised we hadn't paid for the lemons. I knew straight away that I either needed to fess up and return it to one of the workers or go back and pay for it. My values of honesty and inner-harmony forced me to go back to the checkout and pay for the lemon. I could have left the store without paying for it but looking back on it, I think taking the lemon without paying would have made me feel guilty for a long time. I would have spent a long time thinking about it, instead, I made a decision that I can stand by.

Describe how your culture and the people around you have influenced your values and identity.

  • Adventure - While I was growing up, my parents would often bundle my siblings and I in the car and go on an adventure. I remember once we drove to Wellington for a burger. It was a Sunday afternoon, mum wasn't sure what was on the menu so they decided to take us to Wellington to find a good burger for dinner. On our way to Wellington, my parents contacted my aunty and uncle who lived in Wellington and they invited us for dinner. We ended up having the most delicious Spaghetti Bolognese. After dinner we drove back to our house in Ashhurst. These types of adventures were common in our childhood and I believe these activities grew my sense and want for adventure.

  • Humour - Growing up with parents who always found the funny side of things. Family who knew that laughter was the best medicine. Family who didn't mind making toilet jokes at the dinner table. I have fond memories of sitting around the dinner table with my family, all gasping for air as we belly laugh for a few mins, sometimes with tears in our eyes. I strive for laughter/humour like that.

  • Honesty and trustworthiness was a value I learnt early on. I learnt that being truthful (even when it's hard) helps to build strong relationships.I strive to tell the truth to keep myself accountable and to any action I make.

  • Wisdom - Growing up I've learnt that wisdom comes from experience, mistakes and from the people I surround myself with. I have learnt the value of listening to others experiences to gain different perspectives.

  • Peace - My parents are well loved in any community they are part of. While growing up in Ashhurst, this was no different. They always supported those who needed support, they loved those who needed a bit of love, they cared for everyone they knew and they never had anything bad to say about anyone. This taught me the importance of giving back. Because of them, I've learnt to give back to my community and the importance of having a solid community of support.

Identify your strengths and how they will support you during your learning journey.

As mentioned above, my top five strengths (according to Gallup) are Adaptability, Empathy, Positivity, Developer & Includer.
Adaptability will allow me to stay flexible when facing challenges during the course or if my learning environment changes. If things don't go as planned, I will be able to adjust quickly while I find a new approach.
My strength of empathy allows me to connect with people, build strong relationships and be supportive in group situations.
My positivity boosts my resilience in tough times which enables me to keep productive when the course gets tough. I often say "but it's all good, it'll be fine," which is just my way to say, I got this.
I thrive on seeing others (and myself) grow knowledge and skills. I'm always on the look out for opportunties for improvement and I enjoy supporting others to do the same.
I have a desire to include everyone in the task and I don't like anyone feeling like they have been left behind or left out of a conversation. I believe everyone in a group environment should have their voices heard and listened to with intent.

Evaluate your limitations in terms of your learning and career development. How might these affect your learning journey?

One of my biggest challenges is time management. I live a busy life with work, exercise and family commitments. I'm nervous about how I can fit everything in while maintaining a healthy balance. The overwhelming pressure of tasks can easily become too much. Although this course is a welcomed pressure, it will help me to create strong boundaries and I will have to learn to prioritise what is most important.

Another challenge of mine for the year of learning, is the public speaking part of the course - something which I'm anxious about. The thought of having to speak to others about my work is terrifying. I considered pulling out of the course to avoid the speaking in front of others.
Instead, I spoke to a friend who studied with Dev Academy a few years ago and they advised the course really helped him get over his fear of public speaking. If he can do it, then so can I.
When I really think about my workplace or my gym life, once I became comfortable in these spaces speaking to a group became easier - I just need to give myself the time to build confidence in myself and confidence in what I'm talking about.
I may have been super nervous for my first home group meeting, I may have stumbled my words or forgotten what to say. But I believe that the more I do speak publicly (or in this case) over video chat, the more comfortable I will become - fingers crossed. And I know, public speaking is an important part of my future career, so starting small here may be the best way to get over my fear, or at least start to get over it.

Share an example of when you were trying to work productively with others, but there was resistance or tension. Discuss strategies you tried at the time, how effective they were, and your reflections on what other strategies you would try now and why.

As a Service Desk Analyst, I am constantly met with tension from customers when they want their computer fixed NOW and don't have time to allow me to troubleshoot and figure out the best way to resolve the issue. During these situations, I use empathy to my advantage. I say things such as, "I understand your frustration" or "I can see how annoying this must be".
While I could spend my time working through all of the basic troubleshooting steps, I ask the customer specific questions to do with their issue, such as "what did you notice before Outlook crashed?"
Asking specific questions ensure I can never assume what the issue is and it won't allow any time wasting.
As soon as I can in a call, I will remote into their device to have a look at the issue. It's much easier to look at the issue than have the customer try to explain the issue, as they can often miss out important details.

During time of reflection of these situations, I could set time expectations earlier in the call if I know what the issue is or if I'm unsure, I shouldn't be scared to admit that I'm unsure. Or I could even give an explanation of what I'm doing as I'm doing it - this is important when remoted into their device. This will help customers know that I am working on their issue rather than just clicking random buttons on their laptop.
