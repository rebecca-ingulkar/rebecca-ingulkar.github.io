Problem Solving

My reflections on problem solving techniques following completing the JavaScript-Kata challenge

30 March 2025

Blocked by a simple problem

During the JavaScript-Kata challenge, superFlipFlop, I found myself being blocked by my choice of brackets in a console.log call. I was trying to log the function name with an array as the parameter although I had square brackets in the wrong place that was causing the console to log "undefined".
It was frustrating at the time because the square brackets and the curved brackets look so similar and they are easily gazed over when checking code for errors.
I found myself looking at the code, over and over. Reading it out loud - even then, I still couldn't find my error. I turned to google, asking it why my console.log in logging 'undefined'. I saw something online about the bracket format and re-read my code... Duh, my brackets were in the wrong place.
Lightbulb moment!

Elegantly solving a problem

Google defines elegantly as: adjective - graceful and stylish in appearance or manner.
similar words: stylish, suave, swanky, smart, refined, tasteful.

During the JavaScript-Kata challenge, I used my ability to read error messages and googling to elegantly solve the problems that I was facing. I found MDN Web Docs and W3Schools to be rather helpful in explaining different JS methods and I found a handy youtube channel that has beginner JS video tutorials. When I wasn't able to find the suitable help through YouTube and google, I resulted to asking for help in the discord help desk channel.

Problem Solving Techniques

  • Pseudocode

    The use of Pseudocode is a new technique for me, in the past I haven't used Pseudocode to plan out my code. This is something that I need to work and remembering to do it, will be my biggest obstacle.

  • Trying something

    I'm comfortable trying something in the process of problem-solving. I feel like this technique goes hand in hand with using google - sometimes I stumble across a method that could be used to solve the task and I figure I might as well try it in the hope that it works. I know that I can always Ctrl + Z to undo it if it doesn't work!

  • Rubber ducky method

    Although I don't have a physical rubber ducky, I find that I'm often talking out loud to myself while working - either reading out the question/task or reading something I have found online. Hearing myself say what I'm reading helps me to understand what my eyes and brain are trying to comprehend.

  • Reading error messages

    I've found that error messages are often rather helpful. Most of the time, the message tells you which line is incorrect or not working. The error messages sometimes will point to the part of the code that is incorrect, which I think it a total game changer. Imangine if error messages didn't say exactly what was wrong, we'd be stuck on something for days! Error messages are annoying and sometimes they are hard to read but I'm getting there!

  • Console.logging

    Another technique that I don't use as much. I find that I struggle with what to enter inside the brackets and sometimes where to put the console.log() itself - inside or outside the function - that is the question. This is a technique that I need to work on!

  • Googling

    Google is great! As mentioned above, I use Google alot. It's available instantly and it's easy to use. The only downside to google is that sometimes there are too many results and I have to filter through to find exactly what I'm looking for.

  • Asking your peers for help

    I'm not confident with this one but I would like to be. Over the past two sprints, I have enjoyed discussing struggles during the looping challenge - this showed me that we are all in the same boat and I could ask my peers for help if needed.

  • Asking coaches for help

    At first I felt embarrased to ask for help on the Discord help desk page. But seeing others in the cohort asking similar questions gave me the confident to post my own questions. I have come to realise that if I have questions or if I'm stuck on something then it's totally possible for someone else to be stuck and maybe my question will help them too!

  • Improving your process with reflection

    This is another technique I need to work on. I do enjoy reflecting on what I've learnt, but I would like to reflect more about what I can do better next time.

Reflect on a time you were reluctant to ask for help.

During JavaScript-Kata Gradebook challenge. I was stuck on the challenge for the entire time box suggested time of four hours. It was such a frustrating task. I was reluctant to ask for help because the task seemed so simple and I thought that maybe my question was just silly and embarrasing. I took a break, away from the screen and got some fresh air. On my return, I asked for help in the help desk chat and it turns out another cohort member was stuck too! Next time I find myself in the same situation, I will ask for help sooner - being stuck on the same issue for hours on end is not helpful to anybody (especially yourself.)
